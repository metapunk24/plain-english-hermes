## 1. "Was" / "Became" / Rewrite Patterns

### Example 1.1 — Plain English vs. inflated style
**Before:**
> He was conveyed to his place of residence in an intoxicated condition

**After:**
> He was carried home drunk

*(Arthur Quiller-Couch’s classic demonstration of plain English)*

### Example 1.2 — School emergency notice
**Before:**
> In the event of an emergency evacuation of these premises should you require assistance to facilitate your evacuation would you please advise your host or reception on arrival.

**After (rewrite — “could simply have said”):**
> Visitors: if there’s an emergency, will you need help to leave the building? If so, please tell reception or your host now.

### Example 1.3 — Disabled-minibus sticker
**Before:**
> Please leave sufficient space behind this vehicle to allow safe ingress and egress of mechanically propelled, seated position, ambulatory devices, by means of authorized lifting equipment thank you.

**After (rewrite — “In more relaxed English, it could have said”):**
> Leave us a gap! Please leave at least 2 metres (6 feet) clear so we can lift wheelchairs in and out. Thanks.

### Example 1.4 — Abstract charity brief
**Before:**
> The ‘onion model’ … set out … the Government’s vision of what was needed to achieve whole system change. Recent tragic events have demonstrated that there is an urgent need for still greater integration at every layer of the ‘onion’ in front-line delivery, processes, strategy and governance. At the level of service delivery in particular there remains significant practical, philosophical and resource barriers to full integration. Further legislative changes at governance level alone will not automatically make it easier to address these barriers.

**Comment:** Author remarks: *“How could anyone have thought this was publishable? … Studied but not understood — please rewrite in normal English.”*

## 2. Quoted Originals Followed by Improved Versions

### Example 2.1 — Council officer jargon
**Before:**
> The whole hierarchy from waste minimisation through collection, recycling and residual dispersal has spatial implications.

> The diversity of Central Wales’ environment also offers unrealised economic potential if developed sensitively and knowledge based industries, new environmental technologies and sustainable forms of high-quality tourism, both inland and at the coast, can be encouraged.

*(No direct rewrite shown in text, but author notes these make readers “halt, backtrack, and—often—give up.”)*

### Example 2.2 — Tenerife air-crash ambiguity
**Before:**
> at takeoff

*(Used by one flight crew to mean “in the process of taking off”; tower took it to mean “at the takeoff point.” Result: 583 fatalities.)*

**Lesson:** Ambiguous phrasing can be fatal; precision matters.

### Example 2.3 — Police / fire management-speak
**Before:**
> a management conference demountable unit [i.e. a mobile control-room] is on the way from the management resource centre

**Comment:** Coroner asks *“What worries me is all you senior people … are allowing yourselves to be taken over by management jargon… we have to communicate with people in plain English.”*

### Example 2.4 — Heart-attack surgery explanation
**Before:**
> If you have suffered severe damage to your heart muscle, then you may have a physical problem such as a ruptured muscle which is preventing your heart from working in the right way. If this is the case then you should be considered for surgery to put this right within two days of your heart attack. *[56 words, ASL 28]*

**After:**
> If a heart attack has severely damaged your heart muscle, it may be ruptured and not working properly. So your doctor should consider surgery within two days. *[27 words, ASL 13]*

### Example 2.5 — Blockchain description
**Before:**
> The infancy stage is where blockchain currently resides.

**After:**
> Blockchain is in its infancy.

### Example 2.6 — Mortgage-arrears letter (full worked rewrite)
**Before:**
> Arrears at present subsist on your mortgage account in the sum of £1,032, with a further payment becoming due on the 11th April. In view of the account being a mortgage account, we are not in a position to stop interest being debited each month and in order to prevent the account situation from deteriorating, it is necessary that payments are received each month which represent the interest debit. At present this amount is £242 and therefore it is regretted your offer to make payments in the sum of £80 a month is not sufficient.

**After (59 words):**
> The arrears on your mortgage account are £1,032, and a further payment is due on 11 April. Regrettably we cannot stop interest being charged. Therefore, to prevent the arrears growing, you will need to pay the interest charge each month. At present this is £242, so we regret that your offer to pay £80 a month is not sufficient.

**Focus-group result:** 29/34 preferred the revised version; clarity score 17/20 vs. 10/20 for original.

### Example 2.7 — Car-fleet management guide
**Before:**
> This guide would have the objective of highlighting to car fleet managers the best way to achieve, and the benefits of adopting, a professional approach vis-à-vis managing a car fleet. There are only a few publications at present covering the subject of car fleet management and with no current insurance company involvement there would appear to be a definite market niche for us to explore.

**After (second redraft, 48 words):**
> This guide would show car-fleet managers how to do their work more professionally and why this would benefit them. Few publications cover car-fleet management, none of them from insurers. So there’s a market niche for us to exploit.

*(Cut from 72 words to 48 — one-third reduction.)*

### Example 2.8 — Engineering cost statement
**Before:**
> Overestimating on one type of material could have a detrimental cost effect for the clients, depending on the prices in the Bill of Quantities.

**After:**
> Overestimating on one type of material could cost the clients more, depending on the prices in the Bill of Quantities.

### Example 2.9 — Housing-stock transfer
**Before:**
> Between them the group members own the majority of Housing Association owned properties throughout Blackshaw. There are around 5,000 properties that are owned by Housing Associations throughout Blackshaw. The five Associations that are in the Blackshaw Housing Partnership own approximately 4,450 of these properties (89%).

**After (15 words):**
> Between them, the group members own 4,450 of the 5,000 housing association properties in Blackshaw.

### Example 2.10 — Active vs. passive — financial adviser
**Before (passive):**
> We have been asked by your home insurers to obtain your written confirmation that all their requirements have been completed by yourself.

**After (active):**
> Your home insurers have asked us to obtain your written confirmation that you have completed all their requirements.

**Focus group:** 28/35 preferred the active; clarity 17/20 vs. 13/20.

### Example 2.11 — Safety official
**Before (passive):**
> A recommendation was made by inspectors that consideration be given by the company to the fitting of an interlock trip between the ventilation systems to prevent cell pressurisation.

**After (active + vigorous verbs):**
> Inspectors recommended that the company consider fitting an interlock trip between the ventilation systems to prevent cell pressurisation.

*(10 words shorter; 18/35 preferred the final version.)*

### Example 2.12 — Annual benefits statement
**Before (passive):**
> Benefits from contributions invested in the With-Profits Fund are not guaranteed and are dependent on the bonuses declared by us.

**After (active):**
> We can’t guarantee the benefits you’ll get from the contributions we’ve invested on your behalf in the With-Profits Fund. That’s because these benefits will depend on the bonuses we declare.

### Example 2.13 — Vigorous verbs: Indian railway editorial
**Before:**
> Another major disaster so soon after last month’s accident highlights the inexcusable failure of the Railways in ensuring the requisite alertness for precluding a disaster which has claimed so many victims.

**After:**
> Coming so soon after last month’s disaster, this accident proves the railways can’t stay alert enough to prevent such horrors.

*(One-third fewer words.)*

### Example 2.14 — Nominalization → active verb (official letter)
**Before:**
> I have now had sight of your letter to Mr Jones.

**After:**
> I have now seen your letter to Mr Jones.

### Example 2.15 — Nominalization → active verb (business)
**Before:**
> Funding and waste management have a direct effect on progress towards the decommissioning of plant and equipment.

**After:**
> Funding and waste management directly affect progress towards the decommissioning of plant and equipment.

### Example 2.16 — Researchers’ intention
**Before:**
> The original intention of the researchers was to discover the state of the equipment.

**After:**
> The researchers originally intended to discover the state of the equipment.

### Example 2.17 — Expectation
**Before:**
> There is therefore an expectation on our part that the land will be used for building.

**After:**
> So we expect the land will be used for building.

### Example 2.18 — Conservation group
**Before:**
> The conservation group says the department should proceed with the introduction of six mini-recycling centres.

**After:**
> The conservation group says the department should introduce six mini-recycling centres.

### Example 2.19 — Team role
**Before:**
> The team’s role is to perform problem definition and resolution.

**After:**
> The team’s role is to define problems and resolve them.

**Or:**
> The team defines and resolves problems.

### Example 2.20 — Policy branch review
**Before:**
> Our policy branch has carried out a review of our procedures in order to effect improvements in the reporting of accidents.

**After:**
> To improve the reporting of accidents, our policy branch has reviewed our procedures.

### Example 2.21 — Meeting water standards
**Before:**
> Meeting the new lead-in-water standards requires a combination of plumbosolvency reduction and lead pipework replacement operations.

**After (bullet list):**
> To meet the new lead-in-water standards, we are:
> • reducing the level of dissolved lead in the water, and
> • replacing lead pipework.

### Example 2.22 — Insurers notification
**Before (passive):**
> Notification has been received from the insurers that they wish to re-issue the Tenants Scheme Policy.

**After (active):**
> The insurers have notified us they wish to re-issue the Tenants Scheme Policy.

### Example 2.23 — Maintenance records
**Before (passive):**
> An examination of the maintenance records for the plant was carried out by Mrs Adeyoju.

**After (active):**
> Mrs Adeyoju examined the maintenance records for the plant.

### Example 2.24 — Payroll costs
**Before (passive):**
> A reduction in payroll costs can be achieved if payslip printing is no longer required.

**After (active):**
> Payroll costs will fall if payslips are no longer printed.

**Or:**
> If we stop printing payslips, our payroll costs will fall.

### Example 2.25 — Skateboard park feasibility
**Before (heavy nominalization):**
> The committee made a resolution that a study be carried out by officials into the feasibility of the provision of a skateboard park in the area.

**After:**
> The committee resolved that officials should study the feasibility of providing a skateboard park in the area.

**Or:**
> The committee resolved that officials should investigate whether it’s feasible to provide a skateboard park in the area.

### Example 2.26 — Monetary losses
**Before:**
> The incidence of serious monetary losses in several transactions entered into by the firm during the year is causing us great concern.

**After:**
> We’re very concerned about serious monetary losses in several of the firm’s transactions during the year.

**Or:**
> The firm has lost large sums in several transactions this year, which concerns us greatly.

### Example 2.27 — John Lewis complaint email (splitting long sentence)
**Before (79-word sentence):**
> I am sorry to hear that the casing at the back of the pressure washer is broken, please be assured this is not the quality of products we aim to provide at John Lewis and I will certainly feed this back, as the item was delivered directly by our supplier I will have to pass this over to our supplier direct team for them to arrange a replacement with Karcher, they will call you back when this has been done.

**After (4 sentences):**
> I’m sorry that the casing at the back of the pressure washer is broken. Please be assured this is not the kind of quality we aim to provide at John Lewis. As the item was delivered directly by our supplier, I’ll pass this over to our supplier-direct team, who will arrange a replacement with Karcher. The team will call you when this has been done.

### Example 2.28 — Tax-team offer
**Before:**
> Our tax team will track the enquiry and provide you with additional assistance should that be necessary.

**After:**
> Our tax team will track the enquiry and give you more help if needed.

### Example 2.29 — Estate utilisation
**Before:**
> We spend a lot of our shrinking financial envelope on estate that we don’t utilise with sufficient effectiveness.

**After:**
> We spend a lot of our money on buildings we don’t use effectively enough.

### Example 2.30 — Wrong word: amerce
**Before:**
> With bright rhododendrons in bloom and stunning pink primulas towering above unfolding ferns, the gardens are a wonderful place to **amerce** yourself in and explore.

**After (correction):**
> … a wonderful place to **immerse** yourself in and explore.

### Example 2.31 — Wrong word: mitigate
**Before:**
> Increasingly, we live in a post-truth world where those who clamour loudest are indulged—even if this **mitigates** against finding some sort of relief for what is unquestionably a serious and miserable disorder.

**After (correction):**
> … even if this **militate**s against finding some sort of relief …

### Example 2.32 — Removing not-without
**Before:**
> The risk-based approach is **not without** its own well-recognised disadvantages.

**After:**
> The risk-based approach has some well-known disadvantages.

## 4. Conciseness Drill — Quick before/after snippets

| # | Before | After | Saving |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 | The owners need to stop the rain **from coming in**. | The owners need to stop the rain. | "from coming in" redundant |
| Q2 | If a container is fairly watertight, the air inside will prevent it **from sinking** for weeks. | If a container is fairly watertight, the air inside will prevent it **sinking** for weeks. | "from" unnecessary |
| Q3 | **In order to** suspend debate, she used a technicality. | **To** suspend debate, she used a technicality. | "In order" padding |
| Q4 | The oil platforms are **in the process of being** onshored and recycled. | The oil platforms are **being** onshored and recycled. | "in the process of" redundant |
| Q5 | The officials proposed a wide range of **different** policies. | The officials proposed a wide range of policies. | policies are inherently different |
| Q6 | We need to set aside as much money as we **possibly** can, **going forward**. | We need to set aside as much money as we can. | "possibly"/"going forward" redundant |
| Q7 | **At this particular moment of time**, there are several spoil heaps that are **in imminent danger of** collapse. | **At present**, several spoil heaps are **about to** collapse. | 10 words → 6 words |
| Q8 | **There are** too many staff **who are** not pulling their weight. | **Too many** staff **are** not pulling their weight. | "There are" removed |
| Q9 | The expert said the picture was an **exact replica** of mine. | The expert said the picture was a **replica** of mine. | replica implies exact |
| Q10 | They made a **major breakthrough**, and **so therefore** new investment money poured in. | They made a **breakthrough**, and **so** new investment money poured in. | "major" and "therefore" redundant |
| Q11 | **At close of business as of today’s date**, the bank balance was $52,000. | **At close of business today**, the bank balance was $52,000. | "as of today’s date" redundant |
| Q12 | They **repeated the same mistake again**. | They **made the same mistake again**. | "repeated" already means "again"; or "They repeated the same mistake." |

## 5. Vertical-list conversions

### Example 5.1 — Moorside Plan
**Before (dense paragraph):**
> The Moorside Plan is a new way of planning for the future of our area. It will set the framework within which we plan for the future and includes important choices about development within Moorside. It will provide us with a clear vision to guide development and will promote measures to improve our economy, provide the homes we need, and protect our environment.

**After (bulleted list):**
> The Moorside Plan is a new way of planning for the future of our area which will:
> • set the framework in which we plan for the future
> • include important choices about development in Moorside
> • give us a clear vision to guide that development, and
> • promote measures to improve our economy, provide the homes we need, and protect our environment.

### Example 5.2 — Salt-intake advice (parallelism fix)
**Before (mixed grammar):**
> To restrict your salt intake, you should:
> • not add salt at the table
> • use only a little salt in cooking
> • **do not use** bicarbonate of soda or baking powder in cooking
> • avoid salty food like tinned fish, roasted peanuts, olives.

**After (fixed parallel structure):**
> To restrict your salt intake, you should **use only a little salt in cooking. Also, you should not**:
> • add salt at the table
> • use bicarbonate of soda or baking powder in cooking, or
> • eat salty food like tinned fish, roasted peanuts, and olives.

### Example 5.3 — Clerk’s certificate checks (platform statement)
**Before:**
> • You should check that the details on the self-certificate or medical certificate match those on the person’s information card.
> • You should check that the certificate has been completed correctly and conforms to the rules on validity.
> • That the certificate covers the period of absence.

**After:**
> You should check that:
> • the details on the self-certificate or medical certificate match those on the person’s information card
> • the certificate has been completed correctly and conforms to the rules on validity, and
> • the certificate covers the period of absence.

### Example 5.4 — Ms Kanhai’s aims (grammatical parallelism)
**Before (mixed structures):**
> When Ms Kanhai began work, she established the following aims:
> • **make** the regulations simple to understand …
> • **to update** forms, leaflets, and the website …
> • **the effects of competition will be considered**
> • **the creation of** a document summarizing …
> • **recent changes in legislation should be taken into account**.

**After (uniform infinitives):**
> When Ms Kanhai began work, she established the following aims:
> • **To make** the regulations simple to understand and up to date in structure and layout.
> • **To update** forms, leaflets, and the website where necessary with details of current fees.
> • **To consider** the effects of competition.
> • **To create** a document summarizing details of the regulations, which will enable people to focus on key issues and requirements.
> • **To take account of** recent changes in legislation.
